The Moon is Earth’s only natural satellite, orbiting at an average distance of about 384,000 kilometers and profoundly influencing Earth’s tides, rotation, and axial stability.
Formation and Structure
The Moon formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago, likely from debris ejected after a collision between Earth and a Mars-sized body named Theia, shortly after Earth’s formation. It has a differentiated structure with a solid core, mostly solid/partly liquid mantle, and a thin crust, similar to a “chocolate-dipped cherry” model. Unlike Earth, the Moon has a very thin exosphere instead of a thick atmosphere, and its surface is covered with regolith dust created by impacts over billions of years.

Orbit and Rotation
The Moon orbits Earth at an average distance of 384,399 kilometers, revolving around a common center of mass called the barycenter, located about 4,680 kilometers from Earth’s center. Its rotation is synchronized with its orbit (tidal locking), so the same side always faces Earth, while libration allows us to see about 59% of its surface over time. The Moon completes a sidereal month in 27.3 days and a synodic month in 29.5 days, which governs the lunar phases visible from Earth.

Influence on Earth
The Moon’s gravitational pull is the primary driver of Earth’s ocean tides, creating bulges in the oceans and gradually affecting Earth’s rotation, lengthening the day over time. It also contributes to the precession of Earth’s axis, stabilizing the planet’s tilt and climate. Historically, the Moon’s presence has been crucial for timekeeping, mythology, and scientific observation.

Surface Features and Geology
The Moon’s surface is marked by impact craters, maria (dark basalt plains), and highlands, with volcanic activity ceasing about 1.2 billion years ago. Its far side receives sunlight just like the near side, though it is not visible from Earth. Lunar rocks brought back by Apollo missions have provided insight into its composition and history, confirming its origin and evolution.

Human Exploration
Humans first orbited the Moon with Apollo 8 in 1968 and landed on its surface with Apollo 11 in 1969, with twelve astronauts visiting by 1972. Current and future missions, such as NASA’s Artemis program, aim to return humans to the Moon to study its surface, resources, and role in understanding the early solar system.

Summary
The Earth-Moon system is a dynamic and interdependent pair, with the Moon shaping Earth’s tides, rotation, and axial stability, while preserving a record of the early solar system. Its proximity and size make it a unique natural satellite, both scientifically invaluable and historically significant for human exploration and understanding of our planet.
